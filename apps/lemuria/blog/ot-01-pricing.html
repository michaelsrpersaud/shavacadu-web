Off the Trailhead — Salesforce Hot Takes

Salesforce Just Raised Prices Again. Here's What They Don't Say in the Press Release.

📅 June 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 🏷 Pricing & Renewals

When Salesforce ended its seven-year pricing freeze in 2023 with a 9 percent increase, most customers absorbed it. When the company followed with another 6 percent increase in August 2025, finance teams started paying closer attention. When analysts began forecasting a further 5 to 7 percent increase in 2026, some of them started doing the actual math.

The math is worth doing — and not just on the invoice itself.

The Number Buried in the Earnings Report

Analysis of Salesforce's fiscal year 2026 results revealed that price increases contributed approximately 6.3 percentage points to reported annual recurring revenue growth — accounting for up to 72 percent of the year's total ARR gain.

72%

Of Salesforce's FY2026 ARR growth was attributable to price increases on existing customers — not new customer acquisition.

Source: Analysis of Salesforce FY2026 results; 2-data.com, 2026

Let that land. Roughly 72 cents of every new dollar of revenue Salesforce reported in FY2026 came not from bringing new customers onto the platform — but from charging existing ones more. That is not growth in the way the word is traditionally used. That is a monetization strategy dressed in growth language.

To be fair: Salesforce is not doing anything unusual by enterprise software standards. Price discipline is standard practice when you control a platform your customers cannot easily leave. But customers who understand this dynamic negotiate differently than those who do not.

The Price History Everyone Should Know

Salesforce held list prices flat for seven years — a deliberately customer-friendly posture that helped them win deals and lock in multi-year contracts. Then the landscape shifted. In 2023 came the first increase: 9 percent across most editions. In August 2025 came another: 6 percent, primarily affecting Enterprise and Unlimited.

The practical impact: Enterprise licenses moved from $165 to approximately $175 per user per month. Unlimited moved from $330 to approximately $350. At list price, a 500-seat Enterprise org saw its annual bill increase by roughly $60,000 in one cycle — before any discussion of new features or expanded usage.

Analysts at Redress Compliance, tracking the full price increase history, project a further 5 to 7 percent increase in 2026. If accurate, that is three price increases in four years after a seven-year freeze — a pace that compounds quickly at enterprise seat counts.

The Agentforce Pricing Fiasco

The base license increases were manageable. The Agentforce pricing rollout was not.

Salesforce initially introduced Agentforce at $2 per conversation — a simple-sounding number that quickly met with customer confusion and, in many cases, alarm. Enterprise customers running high-volume service operations began calculating what "per conversation" actually meant at their scale. The numbers were not comfortable.

The backlash was significant enough that Salesforce overhauled the pricing model entirely. The company moved toward a bundled approach that offered more predictability — but the initial rollout left a mark on how enterprise buyers perceive Salesforce's instinct when it comes to AI monetization. The message received, rightly or wrongly: we will charge as much as the market will accept, and we will figure out the right number through trial and error.

Forrester Research noted that a quarter of planned 2026 AI spending across the industry might be delayed to 2027 as boards — burned by ambiguous AI pricing models — began demanding clearer ROI before green-lighting new commitments. Agentforce's initial pricing was a case study in how to accelerate that caution.

The Slack Incident

If the Agentforce pricing controversy was uncomfortable, the Slack situation in late 2025 was a public relations disaster.

Reports emerged of Slack demanding $50,000 upfront and a new annual rate of $200,000 from a customer — representing a roughly 40x increase over their prior agreement. The demand was not a negotiating position. It arrived as a take-it-or-leave-it number. When the details became public, the backlash was immediate and loud enough that Slack executives issued a public apology, calling the demand a "mistake."

The Slack incident matters beyond its specifics because Slack is part of the Salesforce ecosystem. For enterprise customers who have integrated Slack deeply into their operations, this was a preview of what ecosystem pricing can look like when the vendor decides the lock-in is strong enough to support aggressive extraction.

What This Means in the Renewal Room

None of this is an argument to leave Salesforce. For most organizations using the platform seriously, the switching cost and operational disruption of migration far exceeds the cost of the price increases — and Salesforce knows it. That is, in fact, the basis of the pricing strategy.

But knowing this changes how you approach the renewal conversation.

Document everything before renewal, not during it. License utilization data, inactive user counts, feature adoption rates — all of it. A customer who can show exactly which features are used, at what scale, by how many active users, is a different negotiating counterpart than one who shows up with a number on an invoice and a vague sense that it feels high.

Understand that list price is a starting position. Most enterprises negotiate 20 to 40 percent below list. The floor moves based on how much data you bring and how credible your alternatives look.

Know where you are actually locked in and where you are not. Some Salesforce products have real competitive alternatives. Others do not. The negotiating leverage is different in each case, and conflating them weakens your position.

Model the full trajectory, not just this year's increase. If 2023 was 9 percent, 2025 was 6 percent, and 2026 is projected at 5 to 7 percent, the compounding effect over a 3-year renewal is material. Price it into the contract conversation now, not at renewal three years from now.

When 72 cents of every new revenue dollar comes from raising prices on existing customers, that is not growth. That is extraction. Understanding the difference changes how you sit at the renewal table.

Salesforce is an exceptional platform with deep capability and, for most organizations, genuine competitive advantage. It is also a vendor that has decided its pricing power warrants sustained increases on a customer base that cannot easily leave. Both of those things are true simultaneously. The organizations that do well in this environment are the ones that walk into every renewal understanding both sides of that equation.
